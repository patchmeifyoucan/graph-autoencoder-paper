In this section, we review methods for graph generation from two branches in the literature.
First, from the point of view of graph generative modeling, we highlight common problems and solutions
when generating graphs.
Second, we review the literature on the more recently proposed graph autoencoders, aim to generate
a particular graph from a latent representation.
There is no strict separation between these two fields as they solve related problems.
The main difference is that graph generative models aim to learn to generate graphs from a particular graph distribution
whereas graph autoencoders aim to generate a particular graphs.
Central to both branches is the exact procedure generating the graphs.
As such, the concept of variational graph autoencoders lies in between these two branches.\\

In the realm of graph generative modeling, there exist two classes of algorithms for graph generation.
The first class generates graphs within a feedforward architecture and must account for the
node order and validity~\cite{graphvae,de2018molgan,edmdiffusion,digress}.
In practice, this often amounts to imposing or implicitly learning a canonicalization to make the reconstruction loss well-defined.
The second class generates graphs sequentially, and as such the node ordering becomes
implicit but needs to introduce additional training and inference procedures~\cite{graphrnn,gran,learning-deep-gen-graphs}
as elements are generated one at a time.

Early work introduces autoencoders~\cite{rumelhart1986learning,baldi1989neural,hinton2006autoencoder}
as a general form of non-linear dimensionality reduction and representation learning.
Standard autoencoders are not constrained in any sense in how they learn a latent representation.
As such, denoising~\cite{vincent2008dae} and contractive~\cite{rifai2011cae} introduce additional restrictions
on the latent space for robustness while sparse~\cite{ng2011sparseae} versions aim for better interpretability.
Specifically for graphs, variational graph autoencoders~\cite{vgae} have been proposed.
However, this early version reconstructs on the node-level rather than graph-level.
A graph-level extension has been proposed in~\cite{graphvae} in which the authors recognize the problematic
setting of decoding graphs.
The authors directly address graph isomorphism by introducing graph matching to find correspondences and to calculate losses.
The work in~\cite{winter2021pigvae} goes one step further and establishes a line of work in which the model learns
a canonical ordering internally, which they justify by the following argument.
While we can represent a graph with a graph-level latent using permutation-invariant encoders, a decoder must, to
learn the reconstruction via point-wise losses, infer the ordering.
However, this model also needs to train a predictor of graph size from the latent.
The work in~\cite{any2graph2024} proposes to view the problem through the lens of Supervised Graph Prediction (SGP).
They seek to develop a permutation invariant distance for graphs and introduce a loss based on Optimal Transport that
is permutation invariant and handles variable-sized inputs.
A notable improvement is that their model, as it fully operates in graph embedding space, can decode arbitrary embeddings
into graphs and as such is able to decode different input modalities to their corresponding graph representation.
Combining the matching strategy of~\cite{winter2021pigvae} and the loss of~\cite{any2graph2024}, the authors of~\cite{grale2025}
propose an architecture operating on both graph and node embeddings, where the graph-level embeddings serve as a latent
representation and node-level embeddings are used for matching, achieving state-of-the-art results.
